Shifting IT Support Left at Enterprise Scale

Reducing repeat IT tickets by 24% at bp through AI-assisted knowledge discovery and content design.

Snapshot
The “too long didn’t read”.

Problem

High volumes of repeat, low-complexity IT tickets driven by poor knowledge findability and low trust in self-serve channels — increasing operational costs and reducing employee productivity.

Outcome

An AI-assisted self-serve experience that improved knowledge discovery, reduced ticket volume, and shifted demand away from high-cost live support.

Measured impact (3 months post-launch)

  • ⬇️ 24% reduction in repeat (L1) tickets.
  • ⬆️ Self-serve resolution rate: 42% → 63%
  • ⬆️ CSAT: 3.6 → 4.4
  • ⬇️ Average resolution time: –22%
  • ⬆️ Agent satisfaction (internal survey): +31%

bp plc

Year / Industry

Lead Product Designer

The business
Context, constraints, why it mattered.

bp’s internal IT service desk was under sustained pressure from a high volume of repetitive, low-complexity queries — think password resets, access requests, and basic “how-to” questions.

Despite a mature ServiceNow platform and extensive knowledge base, employees struggled to find answers independently and defaulted to live support.

Key signals

  • ~40% of service desk tickets were repeatable, low-complexity (L1 and below).
  • 42% of employees failed to resolve issues through existing self-serve channels.
  • Live agent interactions cost ~8x more than successful self-serve resolutions.
  • 78% of employees reported frustration with searching and filtering knowledge content.

This created a compounding problem:

  • Employees lost productivity waiting for support.
  • Agents spent time on low-value work.
  • The business absorbed unnecessary cost-to-serve.

Strategic backdrop

This initiative sat within a broader “Shift Left” transformation roadmap — moving issue resolution as close to the employee as possible to improve efficiency and reduce reliance on L3 live support.

Generative AI was identified as a potential enabler — not to replace agents, but to improve knowledge discovery, summarisation, and confidence in self-serve outcomes.

My role
Responsibilities and contributions.

As the Lead Product Designer, I was responsible for shaping the end-to-end experience strategy and design execution for the MVP.

My core responsibilities

  • Led discovery, research synthesis, journey mapping, interaction design, and usability testing.
  • Defined the MVP scope in partnership with the Product Owner, balancing impact vs technical feasibility.
  • Embedded bp’s design system into the ServiceNow experience, collaborating closely with developers and design engineering.
  • Acted as design authority across the product triad – aligning product, design, and engineering through bi-weekly sprint rituals.
  • Facilitated workshops with ITSM leadership and SMEs to align user needs with business goals.
I worked closely with the PO and Programme Manager to ensure design decisions directly supported cost reduction, productivity gains, and adoption — not just usability improvements.

Discovery and research
Uncovering the insights.

To avoid treating AI as a solution in search of a problem, we invested heavily in understanding why self-serve was failing.

Quantitative analysis (ServiceNow analytics)

Working with the data team, I reviewed historical ticket and search data:

  • 41% of tickets were “how-to” questions already answered in existing knowledge articles.
  • Knowledge articles averaged ~840 words, with significant drop-off before resolution.
  • Search success was inconsistent, reinforcing distrust in results.
  • Employees often escalated after one failed search attempt.
Insight: The issue wasn’t lack of content — it was lack of confidence and clarity.

1-to-1 interviews with SMEs and power users

I conducted 16 one-to-one interviews across the US, Europe, and APAC with a mix of power users, SMEs, and everyday employees.

Key synthesised insights

  1. Trust drives behaviour
    Employees escalated not because answers didn’t exist, but because they didn’t trust search results to be current or relevant.
  2. Long-form content increases escalation
    Dense articles caused cognitive overload and pushed users toward human support.
  3. Chatbots were perceived as blockers
    The current chatbot experience delayed access to help rather than speeding it up.
  4. Lack of visibility caused anxiety
    Once a ticket was raised, users felt disconnected and uninformed about progress.

Stakeholder alignment workshop

I facilitated a discovery workshop with ITSM leadership and SMEs to:

  • Share research findings.
  • Align on business goals.
  • Explore where AI could responsibly add value.

Leadership aligned on using GenAI as a cost-reduction and productivity lever, with a strong emphasis on transparency, governance, and human fallback.

Problem definition
Turning research into a clear design focus.

Bringing together user, agent, and business needs, we defined the core problem as:

Employees lack a fast, trustworthy way to resolve simple IT issues independently, leading to unnecessary escalation to high-cost live support, reduced productivity, and overwhelmed service desk agents.

This problem had three equally important lenses:

  • Employees needed clarity, speed, and reassurance.
  • Agents needed relief from repetitive L1 work.
  • The business needed sustainable cost-to-serve reduction.

Defining what we want to solve.

This initiative directly supported the "Shift Left" strategy.

Ideation
Thinking outside the box.

We explored a wide range of ideas, including:

  • Knowledge base redesign
  • Support channel consolidation
  • Short-form “how-to” videos
  • Proactive Microsoft Teams notification integration

However, given time, budget, and organisational readiness, we focused on a Generative AI-assisted self-serve model that augmented — rather than replaced — existing workflows.

Key trade-offs

  • De-scoped full automation to avoid AI trust and accuracy risks.
  • Prioritised assistive AI with clear escalation paths.
  • Chose proven interaction patterns (Jakob’s Law) over experimental UI, taking inspiration from the public domain.

MVP focus areas

  • AI-powered knowledge search & summarisation.
  • Clear source transparency and confidence cues.
  • Seamless escalation to ticket creation when needed.

The solution
Designing the AI-assisted experience.

Experience principles

  • Transparency over automation — always show sources.
  • Clarity over completeness — concise, actionable answers.
  • Human fallback by default — AI never blocks support.

Information architecture

  • Introduced a sidebar mega-menu, replacing top-heavy navigation.
  • Grouped tasks, tickets, and approvals by employee intent, not system structure.

AI-powered knowledge search

  • Natural-language input with concise summarised answers.
  • Clear links to original knowledge articles for validation.
  • One-click escalation to ticket creation.
  • Reassurance microcopy to manage expectations and trust.

Accessibility & inclusion

  • WCAG 2.1 AA colour contrast compliance
  • Keyboard-navigable menus
  • ARIA labelling and alt text across components

Result: No critical accessibility issues identified during testing.

Testing & validation
Shipping the MVP.

We tested a high-fidelity Figma prototype mapped closely to ServiceNow capabilities and using the following methods:

Usability study

  • 8 moderated usability sessions.
  • Key tasks: find an answer, raise a ticket, approve a request.

Early sentiment (Net Promoter Score)

Initial NPS: 12.5

  • Promoters praised clarity and speed.
  • Detractors wanted faster responses and clearer exit routes.
This validated direction while highlighting areas for iteration.

Continuous feedback loop

  • Internal design–development testing.
  • Phased validation of LLM outputs and response quality.
  • Rapid triage and iteration during sprints.

Key learning: AI alone wasn’t enough

While AI summarisation improved scanability, it did not consistently increase self-serve success on its own. In many cases, poor outcomes were traced back to the quality and structure of the underlying knowledge base rather than the LLM.

Actions taken:

  • Audited high-traffic knowledge articles feeding the LLM.
  • Partnered with SMEs and content designers to refactor content into: Task-based steps, clear prerequisites, plain-language outcomes.
  • Introduced lightweight “AI optimised" content standards.

Result

  • AI answer usefulness ratings improved by ~15%
  • Repeat escalation for refined queries decreased measurably.

Impact & outcomes
What changed because of this work.

Within three months of launch:

  • ~1,200 repeat tickets avoided.
  • Increased self-serve resolution rate from 42% → 63%
  • Improved CSAT scores from 3.6 → 4.4
  • Reduced resolution time by -22%
  • Increased agent satisfaction: +31%
Based on observed reductions in escalation and average handling time, we estimated a productivity opportunity of ~15–20k employee hours annually.

Reflection
Learnings and key takeaways.

What worked

  • Trust in AI is primarily a design problem, not a technical one.
  • Plain language delivered more impact than additional features.
  • Early service desk buy-in was critical to adoption.

What didn’t

  • AI response latency needs further optimisation.
  • Complex, contextual queries still require refinement. GIGO.

What I’d do next

  • Introduce personalised support journeys.
  • Continuously measure AI answer accuracy over time.
  • Further refine human-AI collaboration across the support lifecycle.
This project reinforced my shift from designing interfaces to designing operational systems — where experience, behaviour, and cost are inseparable.
